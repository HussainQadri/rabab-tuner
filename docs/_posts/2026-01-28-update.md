I have started working on implementing the YIN algorithm in my project, but before I could do so in my tuner_core.py, I needed a prototype that worked with artificial 'dirty' signals and audio as wav files that I could feed into it. 

The YIN algorithm fundamentally works on the ACF, which it adapts into the difference function. The difference function, when expanded and simplified turns out to be the sum of ACFs, which makes implementation easier. However, why do we make the switch to the difference function in the first place? Unfortunately, the ACF may choose too low of a lag (which makes sense, as the smaller lag will maximise the similarity between the lagged signal and the original).

Instead, we can decide on a different approach which takes onto the difference function. This function starts with a simple defintion: The difference between the original signal and the signal with a time shift of T where T is the period must equal 0. Using this fundamental definition, we can square and take the sum, both valid operations that leave the result as 0. This is the difference function. As said before, this can be expressed as the sum of ACF terms.

We aim to find the minima produced by this function, getting 0 is unrealistic. This gives us an approximate period which can be used to find the frequency of the signal. 

The next step as listed on the paper, I am unable to fully grasp at the moment and seems like magic to me. 

The difference function will be 0 at zero-lag and very close to 0 at the actual period because real signals are not perfectly periodic. We can set a lower limit on the search range, but because of resonance, there may be minima that are even lower than the minima at the true period. Essentially, plotting the difference function and finding the global minima doesn't guarantee the true period.
